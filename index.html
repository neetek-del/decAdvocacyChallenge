The Business Problem üéØ

Patrick runs a network of 5 auto repair shops. He‚Äôs been tracking productivity data (number of cars fixed per day) across all shops, along with whether he (the boss) was present at each shop on each day.

The Core Question: When and where should Patrick schedule his presence to maximize productivity and revenue?

Key Considerations:

  • Patrick can only be at one shop per day
  • Different shops may respond differently to his presence
  • Revenue depends on number of cars fixed (assume a fixed price per car)
  • Historical patterns may not predict future performance perfectly
  • Patrick loves visiting his brother who runs Shop 3
ImportantüéØ The Key Insight: Data-Driven Decision Making

The problem: Patrick needs to make scheduling decisions based on data, not intuition. He needs to understand:

  • Which shops benefit most from his presence?
  • Are there patterns by shop that suggest different strategies?
  • What are the revenue and profit implications of different scheduling scenarios?
  • How confident can he be in these recommendations?

Why this matters: Poor scheduling decisions can lead to:

  • Wasted time (Patrick at shops that don‚Äôt need him)
  • Lost revenue (shops that need him but don‚Äôt get him)

The connection: This is a real-world decision problem where data analysis can directly impact business outcomes. Your job is to make the data tell a clear, actionable story.

Confidence, limits, and plain-language caveats

Sample size: 250 days total across 5 shops means about ~50 days per shop on average. That‚Äôs not huge ‚Äî results are informative but uncertain.

Variance: Daily productivity varies for many reasons (customer arrivals, parts, weather, holidays). We capture only a fraction of that variability.

Causality vs correlation: We observe higher or lower productivity on days you were present, but that doesn‚Äôt prove your presence caused the difference. There might be patterns (e.g., you visit busier days) that confound the result. The bootstrap intervals help show how strong the evidence is.

What could go wrong: If your presence is correlated with busy days, the uplift could be overstated. Conversely, if you tend to visit shops on slow days to help, we could understate your effect.

What we did to be careful: bootstrapping to estimate uncertainty, showing all data points, and keeping the analysis simple and transparent so Patrick can understand the assumptions.
